Notes on Execution

On the skill of getting stuff done

Obama said the best career advice he ever gives is simple: "just learn how to get stuff done." ▶ Play YouTube Clip

Getting stuff done – Execution – is what these notes are about.

3 top-level insights:

  1. Getting stuff done is an exceptionally valuable skill. Even Obama said it!
  2. Execution is a learnable skill and we should all spend more time learning it. You can probably name three strategy books/writings, but can you name one on Execution? Exactly!
  3. Great organizations have an execution culture. Organizations that execute well have norms and expectations that enable and demand good execution. The opposite is true too!

After having led a few complex projects at work and read a few Execution-focused books outside of work, here are my notes. This is mostly a documentation of personal insights, but I'd be eager to hear if any of it is useful to others: I'm at GarenCheckley@gmail.com.

▶ The Basics

What is Execution

"Vision without execution is just a hallucination" – Tomas Edison, or Steve Jobs, or someone else smart

Execution is the skill of getting things done well with others in messy, real-world team settings. The "with others" is critical. Getting things done in a team environment means dependencies on other people and groups delivering – and almost everyone is working on multiple things at once. This is what sets the art of execution apart from productivity hacks like how to organize to-do lists, manage calendars, and avoid procrastination.

The Benefits of Execution

When teams execute well:

  • You get results. Obviously!
  • Team members are happier. The best days at work are when meaningful things get done (and people get recognized for it). Most people hate moving too slowly!
  • You actually learn. Learning happens from doing, not planning to do.
  • Users & customers benefit. Things ship. Problems are fixed.

The above ends up creating positive feedback loops – business results compound, happy colleagues make work happier, learning unlocks more learning, and satisfied customers stick around & tell their friends.

The Anti-Execution Feedback Loop

Poor execution results in the inverse of good execution (failure to deliver results, unhappy colleagues, lack of learning, and frustrated customers). But interestingly, that's not all... Poor execution environments create negative feedback loops, resulting in even worse execution:

  • People take on too many projects. This is because any single project goes too slowly or has a high chance of failure, so it's in everyone's interest to keep multiple irons in the fire. But this spreads people too thin and reduces their stake in each product right.
  • People lower their standards. If people can't get stuff done easily they lower their standards to just to get anything done. This encourages peers to do the same, since everyone acknowledges it's nearly impossible to get great stuff done.
  • Progress is marked by showing plans, not results. And leadership rewards this.
  • Good people leave. Because they want to get good stuff done and learn by doing so. Brain drain itself discourages good people from staying!
